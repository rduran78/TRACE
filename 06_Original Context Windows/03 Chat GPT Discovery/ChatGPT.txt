The approach you ended up using — representing spatial relationships as a graph/network and propagating attributes through fixed adjacency structures — is extremely common across computational science, though often under different names.

Your implementation is essentially:

sparse graph neighborhood aggregation

This general architecture appears in many fields.

In GIS and spatial econometrics:

spatial weights matrices,
contiguity matrices,
Moran’s I,
spatial lag models,
SAR/CAR models,
local spillover analysis.

There, regions/cells are nodes and adjacency defines influence/spatial dependence.

In transportation and infrastructure:

road networks,
power grids,
water systems,
logistics routing.

The topology is fixed, while flows/loads vary over time.

In computer vision and remote sensing:

raster convolution kernels,
image segmentation,
object detection,
terrain smoothing,
edge detection.

These are mathematically related because kernels are local neighborhood operators over fixed topology.

In graph theory and network science:

PageRank,
diffusion processes,
shortest paths,
centrality measures,
contagion models,
social networks.

The core idea is:

values propagate across edges

In graph neural networks (GNNs):

node embeddings,
message passing,
graph convolutional networks (GCNs),
spatial-temporal graph learning.

Your algorithm is actually very close conceptually to GNN message passing:

for each node:
    collect neighbor values
    aggregate them

except you are using deterministic aggregation (max, mean, min) instead of learned neural transformations.

In computational physics:

finite element methods,
lattice models,
diffusion systems,
cellular automata.

Again:

topology fixed,
state evolves.

In epidemiology:

disease transmission networks,
spatial contagion,
mobility diffusion.

In economics:

trade networks,
input-output networks,
regional spillovers,
interbank contagion,
migration networks.

In recommendation systems:

user-item graphs,
collaborative filtering,
neighborhood recommendation algorithms.

Even many database systems internally optimize joins through graph-like adjacency representations.

What is interesting in your case is that you arrived at a computational architecture similar to modern graph computation frameworks without explicitly setting out to build one.

Your pipeline now effectively does:

Static graph:
    cells + neighbor edges

Dynamic attributes:
    yearly socioeconomic variables

Operation:
    neighborhood aggregation over sparse topology

That is a very general computational paradigm.

The important conceptual leap is:
you stopped thinking of the data as rows in a table and started thinking of it as a structured relational topology.
